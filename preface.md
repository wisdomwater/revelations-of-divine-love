## Preface

Some books explain God.

Others help us meet Him.

For more than six hundred years, *Revelations of Divine Love* has quietly done the latter.

Julian of Norwich lived in fourteenth-century England, a world marked by uncertainty, violence, plague, and death. She witnessed the devastation of the Black Death, political unrest, and a society where suffering was an ordinary companion of daily life. The Church was the center of spiritual life, yet it was also a time when many believers experienced God primarily through the language of judgment, penance, and fear. Eternal destiny was seldom far from anyone's mind.

It was into that world that Julian received a series of visions while gravely ill, believing she was near death. After recovering, she spent years praying over what she had seen. Rather than rushing to explain her experiences, she allowed them to mature through decades of contemplation. The result became *Revelations of Divine Love*, the first known book in English written by a woman.

What makes Julian remarkable is not simply that she had visions. Throughout Christian history, many people have claimed extraordinary spiritual experiences. What makes Julian enduring is the astonishing consistency of what she saw. Every revelation, every question, every mystery returned to the same center.

Love.

Not sentimentality.

Not wishful thinking.

Not a denial of sin or suffering.

But the unwavering conviction that beneath all things—and working through all things—is the inexhaustible love of God.

For someone like me, those words came as living water.

I grew up in a sincere Christian tradition that emphasized reverence for God. Yet beneath that reverence often lived something else: the quiet fear that God's patience might eventually wear out. I carried the sense that every failure placed me a little closer to His disappointment. My spiritual life became one of constant self-examination, measuring, correcting, confessing, and trying harder. The language of church was filled with doctrine, repentance, humility, holiness, and obedience. These are all good and necessary things. But when they become detached from the overwhelming goodness of God, the soul can become weary. Instead of resting in grace, it begins policing itself. Instead of confidence, it learns vigilance. Instead of freedom, it quietly carries shame.

Julian does not discard repentance. She simply places it where it belongs—inside a love that never fails.

Again and again she insists that God looks upon us with pity rather than blame, that our falls cannot separate us from His love, that Christ is nearer to us than we imagine, and that even our deepest wounds become places where divine mercy is revealed. Her words do not make sin smaller. They make God's love larger. Reading Julian feels less like listening to a theologian constructing arguments and more like sitting beside a wise elder who has spent a lifetime gazing upon the kindness of Christ.

That is why this adaptation exists.

The language of the original is beautiful, but it can also feel distant to modern readers. Medieval English, unfamiliar expressions, and long, intricate sentences sometimes conceal the extraordinary tenderness of Julian's message. My hope has been to preserve her voice while removing those barriers. These daily readings are not intended to replace the original work, but to accompany it—to allow Julian's insights to be heard as though she were speaking gently to us today.

You will notice that these meditations are written in the first person. This was intentional. Rather than repeatedly saying, "Julian teaches..." or "Julian says...," I have tried to let her speak directly to the reader, translating not merely her words but the living spirit behind them. Every effort has been made to remain faithful to her theological vision while expressing it in contemporary language.

We live in another anxious age.

Our fears may not always be those of fourteenth-century England, but they are no less real. We worry about the future. We carry shame over our failures. We wonder if we are enough. We struggle to believe that we are fully known and fully loved. We long for certainty in a world that often feels unstable.

Julian's answer is not an argument.

It is an invitation.

She invites us to behold Christ until His love becomes more convincing than our fear. She teaches us to see our weakness without despair, to trust before we understand, to hope beyond appearances, and to believe that God's deepest purpose is always healing.

If these pages help quiet your anxious heart...

If they help you trust a little more deeply...

If they help you hear, perhaps for the first time, the kindness in God's voice...

...then they will have fulfilled their purpose.

And if, after reading them, one sentence from Julian remains with you above all the others, I hope it is the one she herself waited fifteen years to understand:

*Love was His meaning.*
